About Me

PhD in Exoplanetary Climate Modeling | Simulating Alien Worlds

I am a recent PhD graduate in Astronomy from the University of Leeds (UK). My research mainly focused on simulating Earth-like exoplanet climates using the CESM2-WACCM6 Earth system model. My thesis explored how orbital configurations—such as eccentricity and tidal locking—affect planetary climate, habitability, and observational signatures.

Before my PhD, I earned an MSc in Astrophysics from Queen Mary University of London and a BSc in Astronomy and Mathematics from the University of British Columbia in Canada. My academic interests span planetary science, atmospheric dynamics, and data-driven modeling.

Currently based in Vancouver, I am transitioning into data science and educational roles. I'm particularly excited about applying research-level analytical thinking and simulation tools to real-world problems. I’m also passionate about science communication, interdisciplinary collaboration, and supporting diversity in STEM.

Highlighted Research Projects

Higher Water Loss on Earth-like Exoplanets in Eccentric Orbits

Published in MNRAS (Sep 2023): Demonstrated that orbital eccentricity can significantly raise global temperatures and increase water vapor escape. [Read paper]

Eccentric Orbits May Enhance the Habitability of Earth-like Exoplanets

Published in MNRAS (Aug 2024): Found that moderate eccentricity can extend habitable zones by distributing heat and reducing ice cover. [Read paper]

Climate Simulation of Proxima Centauri b

Published in White Rose eTheses Online (Mar 2025) for the 3rd science chapter of my PhD thesis. Simulating a tidally locked Earth-like world: Proxima Centauri b. Despite the cold average temperature, the planet supports a liquid water region at the substellar point. [Read paper]
